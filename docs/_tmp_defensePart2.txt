Problem Statement



   HVAC control is inherently multi-objective: comfort, energy, and safety must be balanced
   at the same time.
   Purely model-based methods are often accurate but slow or difficult to deploy online.
   Purely model-free RL is flexible, but BOPTEST training is expensive and
   sample-inefficient.
   The central question of this project is therefore:
   Can we build a fast and physically informed RL pipeline that is accurate enough for
   control, scalable enough for experimentation, and structured enough for safe deployment?

Research Roadmap



   Phase 0: establish PPO-based energy-management baseline and identify reward-scaling
   and observation-space issues.
   Phase 1: build a physics-informed RC Neural ODE surrogate to replace slow BOPTEST
   rollouts.
   Phase 2: calibrate the thermal model through an inverse problem to improve physical
   consistency.
   Phase 3: test increasingly structured control architectures, culminating in HDRL, real
   weather, emergency logic, and now the current direct-TSup continuation.

Phase 0: Initial MORL Baseline


    Initial PPO-based baseline established the first Pareto trade-off between comfort and
    energy.
    Two critical issues were discovered:
        scale dominance in the scalarized reward,
        observation-space mismatch between code and environment.
    After fixing these, the early baseline achieved a reported 28% energy reduction and
    produced the first usable Pareto front.
    Phase 0 was important because it revealed that most early instability was not algorithmic,
    but representational.

Phase 1: Why Neural ODE?



   BOPTEST is accurate but slow; large RL sweeps directly on the simulator are impractical.
   The solution was to learn a surrogate with a physically interpretable thermal update.
   The implemented model is an RC Neural ODE with two heads:
       a thermal head predicting ∆T ,
       a power head predicting total HVAC power.
   This preserves the physical meaning of the state transition while remaining fast enough for
   massive batched rollouts.

Neural ODE Formulation
The current surrogate implementation in surrogate/rc_node_v2.py uses the feature vector

                            xt = [T̃zone , T̃amb , sin h, cos h, sin d, cos d, a0 , a1 ].

It predicts:
                                      ∆Tt = fθ (xt ),           Pt = gϕ (xt ),
and updates temperature as
                                           Tt+1 = clip(Tt + ∆Tt ).

Key design choices:
     residual connection in the heat network,
     cyclic encodings for hour and day,
     explicit power positivity via Softplus,
     multi-step training loss with physical regularization.

Phase 2: Inverse-Problem Calibration


    The inverse problem stage addressed parameter mismatch between the nominal thermal
    model and the observed building response.
    Two key issues were solved:
        latency and ill-posed preprocessing,
        weak linear calibration.
    Reported improvement:
        RMSE reduced from 9.12 to 1.91 ◦ C,
        79% error reduction,
        thermal capacitance error reduced to 7.9%.
    This phase made the surrogate more physically trustworthy, which matters later for safety
    and for control transfer.

Phase 3 in the Final Report


According to MorlProjectFinalv3.pdf, Phase 3 consisted of ten systematic experiments.
Main reported findings:
    HDRL specialization improved over flat PPO.
    Real weather integration removed a major simulator-to-controller mismatch.
    Emergency heating solved many extreme cold failures.
    Reward shaping alone amplified the gap instead of closing it.
    BOPTEST fine-tuning did not solve the remaining issue, indicating a structural mismatch
    rather than a pure distribution shift.

Why Phase 3 Had to Continue

The final report itself documented that the remaining performance gap was structural.
The root-cause analysis highlighted three facts:
    weather mismatch was solved,
    extreme cold events were partly solved,
    the remaining gap came from action-response mismatch.

This directly motivated the current continuation:
    move from indirect thermostat-style control to direct supply-air control,
    unify surrogate training, PPO training, and BOPTEST evaluation under the same action
    semantics,
    re-establish strong baselines before re-launching MORL.

Current Redesign: Direct Supply-Air Control

The current continuation changes the control interface fundamentally.
Previous logic:
    indirect thermostat/setpoint control,
    fixed or semi-fixed supply-air behavior,
    higher risk of mismatch between surrogate and BOPTEST.
New logic:
    direct control of TSup in the range [18,35] ◦ C,
    direct fan command,
    wide heating/cooling setpoints in BOPTEST to keep the RL controller in charge,
    identical action semantics in surrogate and BOPTEST backends.

Why the PI Baseline Matters



   The standard controller is not the best comfort controller for a strict 22 ◦ C target.
   However, it is the most energy-efficient reference available in the current benchmark.
   Therefore, it should be interpreted as:
        the low-energy reference,
        not the main apples-to-apples comfort baseline.
   This gives the project a more honest evaluation frame:
        PI baseline = energy reference,
        thermostatic PPO = comfort reference,
        HDRL/MORL = trade-off controllers.

Current Thermostatic PPO Baseline


The current comfort-first baseline is implemented in training/train_thermostatic.py.
Main design choices:
    training on the direct-TSup surrogate rc_node_v3_tsupply.pt,
    17-dimensional observation,
    direct ambient-aware reward centered on the exact target 22 ◦ C,
    weak energy penalty only near the target,
    10 million PPO timesteps with 32 parallel environments.
This controller is intentionally optimized for comfort, not for energy minimization.

Current HDRL Design

The current HDRL implementation in training/train_hdrl.py uses:
    two PPO experts trained on the direct-TSup surrogate,
    a compact 5-feature observation:

                                 [Tzone , CO2 , Ptotal , TSup,prev , Tamb ],

    winter and summer specialization,
    real weather-based domain randomization,
    emergency heating in evaluation when Tamb < 5 ◦ C and Tzone < 20 ◦ C.
The meta-controller currently switches by a simple ambient threshold:

                          Tamb < 12◦ C ⇒ πwinter ,           otherwise πsummer .

Why HDRL Is Still Worse than the Thermostatic Baseline

   Different objective:
        thermostatic PPO is comfort-first and tracks a fixed target of 22 ◦ C,
        HDRL optimizes comfort + energy over the broader band [21,25] ◦ C.
   Different information budget:
        thermostatic PPO uses 17 features including forecasts and history,
        HDRL uses only 5 current-state features.
   Different control logic:
        HDRL relies on a hard seasonal switch at 12 ◦ C,
        this is too crude for shoulder seasons and mixed thermal regimes.
   Result:
        HDRL may be acceptable as a structured controller,
        but it is not yet better than the comfort-only baseline.

Safety Filter Layer

The repository also contains a surrogate-based safety filter in
layers/safety/action_filter.py.
Logic:
    take PPO action,
    rollout the Neural ODE surrogate for a short horizon,
    check whether predicted temperatures stay inside a shrunk safe band,
    if unsafe, replace the action with a fallback controller.

In the current code:
    the horizon is typically 2–4 steps,
    a safety margin is used to compensate surrogate uncertainty,
    evaluation script: evaluation/eval_safe_morl.py.

Current Limitations



    The current HDRL controller does not yet beat the thermostatic PPO baseline on the
    annual comfort-energy trade-off.
    The PI baseline remains far more energy-efficient because it solves a softer
    scheduled-comfort problem.
    MORL has not yet been fully re-trained and re-evaluated on the direct-TSup pipeline.
    The current HDRL gate is threshold-based rather than learned.
    The current annual benchmark uses 12 representative windows of 336 hours each rather
    than a full continuous-year rollout.

Next Steps



   Retrain MORL first on the direct-TSup surrogate, then fine-tune on BOPTEST.
   Replace hard seasonal switching with a learned gate or hysteresis-based meta-controller.
   Give HDRL and MORL the same richer temporal context as the thermostatic PPO
   baseline.
   Move from flat band-based comfort toward a target-aware comfort metric around 22 ◦ C.
   Extend evaluation toward PMV/PPD and ASHRAE 55 style comfort metrics.
   Prepare edge deployment and real-building sensor integration after final controller
   stabilization.

Final Takeaways


   Phase 0 established the MORL baseline and exposed representation problems.
   Phase 1 delivered the key computational breakthrough: a fast and accurate Neural ODE
   surrogate.
   Phase 2 improved physical fidelity through inverse calibration.
   Phase 3 established that hierarchical structure, weather realism, and emergency logic
   matter, but also revealed a structural action mismatch.
   The current continuation solved that mismatch by moving to direct supply-air control.
   Today, the strongest validated result is the direct-TSup thermostatic PPO baseline, while
   HDRL is an operational but not yet dominant trade-off controller.
   The repository is now technically ready for the final MORL step.
